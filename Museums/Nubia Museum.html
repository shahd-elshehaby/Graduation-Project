in Aswan

The museum was conceived during UNESCO’s 1960 campaign to save Nubian monuments, aiming to preserve Nubia’s heritage. Designed by Dr. Mahmoud El-Hakim, it opened in 1997 with a display by Pedro Ramírez Vázquez.

Most Popular Pieces

A model of a Nubian bride's henna night

The museum features numerous models that vividly portray the cultural traditions and daily life of the Nubian people, offering a glimpse into their rich heritage.

The statue of King Ramses II

It's one of the first and most significant pieces displayed at the Nubian Museum, was discovered at the Gerf Hussein temple in Nubia, 160 km south of Aswan. Carved from sandstone, the 8-meter tall statue depicts the king standing, wearing a kilt and the double crown, holding a flail and scepter, with the name "Ramsesu" inscribed on his arm. Ramses II, a prominent 19th Dynasty pharaoh and son of Seti I, ruled for 67 years, engaged in wars with the Hittites, notably the Battle of Kadesh, and built famous temples like Abu Simbel, where sunlight aligns on his statue twice a year (February and October). He was buried in the Valley of the Kings.

The statue of Prince Horemakhet

He is the son of King Shabaka and High Priest of Amun, is considered one of the most beautiful statues in the Nubian Museum in Aswan. Dating back to the 25th Dynasty
(701-690 BC), it is made of quartzite and depicts Horemakhet as the son of King Shabaka and a prominent official during the reigns of Kings Taharqa and Tanutamun. The statue was previously displayed in the Egyptian Museum in Cairo before being moved to the Nubian Museum.

The head of the statue of King Shebitku

He is the son of King "Piye," made of pink granite, was discovered in Karnak. King Shebitku engaged in wars with the Assyrians, and one of the most famous battles was the Battle of Eltekh in 701 BC, where he triumphed over the Assyrian threat.

A model of the original structure of the Deffufa in Kerma

The Deffufa, dating back to 2500 BCE (4,500 years ago), is considered the oldest massive building in Africa. This ancient monument reflects the architectural and cultural significance of the Kerma civilization.

The statue of Harwa

The Great Steward of the Divine Adoratrice of Amun during the 25th Dynasty (Third Intermediate Period), was found in his tomb on the West Bank of Luxor. The statue depicts Harwa seated in an unconventional pose, similar to that of the physician Nyankhre, with a bald head and facial features indicating old age. It is now housed in the Nubian Museum in Aswan.

The statue of King Khafre

It carved from diorite sourced from quarries west of Toshka, depicts him seated on a throne. This iconic representation showcases the king in a regal pose, symbolizing his authority and divine connection. The use of durable diorite highlights the craftsmanship and significance of royal statuary in ancient Egypt.

The lantern of Prince Qawsun

It is made of gilded glass enameled with mina, reflects Mamluk style. Prince Qawsun, a prominent Mamluk of Sultan Al-Nasir Muhammad ibn Qalawun, served as his cupbearer. The lantern features Quranic verse
"Allahu nooru alssamawati waalardi" which means
(Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth) in Naskh script, separated by circular floral motifs on its neck. The body displays plant-based inscriptions with handles for hanging and intricate decorations, including a peacock-like bird and a band with red-colored fish swimming in various directions.

The statue of Isis nursing Horus

It is carved from schist and dating to the Late Period, depicts the goddess seated on a throne, wearing a long dress, a crown with a solar disk and cow horns, and a uraeus on her forehead. She cradles Horus, offering him her breast, symbolizing motherhood. Revered as the wife of Osiris and mother of Horus in Egypt, Isis also held great significance in Kush, reflecting her widespread cultural importance.

Activities

27st of November, 2024

Celebration of the 27th Anniversary of the Nubian Museum’s Opening The Nubian Museum organized a celebration marking 27 years since its opening, attended by directors of various institutions in Aswan and school students. The event featured an exhibition of archival photos documenting the museum’s construction phases, followed by a welcoming speech by Mrs. Thanaa Hassan Moussa, the museum’s Director General, highlighting its role in promoting archaeological awareness. A documentary film was screened, summarizing the museum’s construction stages and achievements over the years. Retired colleagues were also honored for their contributions. The celebration concluded with a choral performance by children from youth and sports centers in Aswan.

1st of January, 2025

The Nubian Museum, in collaboration with the Cultural Palaces Authority, celebrated the International Day of Persons with Disabilities. The event, organized by the Museum Education Department for People with Special Needs, included students from schools for the blind and deaf in Aswan. Activities included art workshops, a general knowledge competition, and performances by students, such as singing, religious chants, and poetry. The event concluded with a performance by the Aswan Cultural Palace Children’s Choir. The celebration was supervised by Mr. Thanaa Hassan, Director General of the Museum, and Dr. Heba Abdel Aziz, Head of the Museum Education Department for People with Special Needs.
