Most Popular Pieces

The statue of the god Nilus

The statue of the god "Nilus" is a three-dimensional sculptural piece, categorized under human/deity statues, dating back to the Greco-Roman era, specifically the 2nd century AD. Discovered in Lower Egypt, Alexandria, at El-Mahmoudia (Sidi Bishr), the statue is made of marble and measures 43 cm in height, 60 cm in length, and 12 cm in width. It was temporarily displayed at the Antiquities Museum of the Bibliotheca Alexandrina starting in 2008 until its return to the Graeco-Roman Museum in Alexandria on August 16, 2022.

Bust of the God Serapis

The marble bust of the god Serapis, dating to the
Greco-Roman period (332 BC – 395 AD), was discovered in Alexandria. Temporarily exhibited at the Bibliotheca Alexandrina's Antiquities Museum from 2008, it was returned to the Greek-Roman Museum on August 16, 2022.

Marble Statue of Marcus Aurelius

Depicting Marcus Aurelius as a Crown Prince or Caesar, this statue presents him in military attire, holding a sword in his left hand while his right rests on a cornucopia, symbolizing the abundance and prosperity of the Roman Empire. Dating to the Roman Period (155–161 AD), it was discovered in Alexandria and is made of marble.

Colossal Head of Octavius

This marble head, dating to the Graeco-Roman Period
(31 BCE – 395 CE), is a remarkable example of sculpture in the round. Standing 75 cm tall, its provenance remains unknown. Temporarily exhibited at the Bibliotheca Alexandrina Antiquities Museum from 2007, it was returned to the Graeco-Roman Museum in Alexandria on August 16, 2022.

The statue of the Apis

The statue of the Apis Bull was commissioned for sculpting by the Roman Emperor Hadrian in the Serapeum of Alexandria, after the Jews destroyed the Serapeum temple, which was originally founded by the Ptolemies and preserved by the Romans. It was discovered in 1895 and is currently displayed at the Greek-Roman Museum in Alexandria.

Statue of King Ptolemy XII

This statue depicts King Ptolemy XII in the traditional Egyptian style, with his left foot forward and arms by his sides, portraying him as a pharaoh. It was discovered at Tebtunis in Fayoum, Egypt.

The statue depicts a Ptolemaic king

On 4th of May 2009, in Shallalat Gardens of Alexandria, a marble statue was found, during the excavations held by H.R.I.A.C. (Hellenic Research Institute of Alexandrian Civilization). The statue represents a standing naked man in a form of classical contraposto, with one foot raised, possibly bent to a support. Head and body are in a very good condition, but the part of the legs under the knees is missing.

Head of Emperor Vespasian

This head depicts Emperor Vespasian with his distinct features but with a younger appearance. It was likely sculpted in Alexandria, where it was originally completed with plaster or an additional marble piece featuring long locks of hair on the right side. This suggests that the head may have originally represented a previous emperor, possibly Caligula or Nero, before being reworked to depict Vespasian.

The statue of the goddess Aphrodite

The statue of the goddess "Aphrodite" is a three-dimensional sculptural piece, categorized under human/deity statues, dating back to the Greco-Roman era, specifically the 2nd century AD. Discovered in Lower Egypt, Alexandria, at El-Mahmoudia (Sidi Bishr), the statue is made of marble and stands 103 cm tall. It was temporarily displayed at the Antiquities Museum of the Bibliotheca Alexandrina starting in 2008 until its return to the Graeco-Roman Museum in Alexandria on August 16, 2022.

Activities

The team of the General Administration of Museum Education for People with Special Needs at the Graeco-Roman Museum welcomed a group of our children with special needs, including those with intellectual disabilities and learning difficulties. The day included the following activities: A guided tour to introduce them to the museum's unique collections, accompanied by simplified explanations. A workshop for designing animated drawings using cardboard, where the children animated artworks inspired by pieces from the museum. A storytelling session using these drawings, which the children greatly enjoyed. They also took commemorative photos to document their experience.
Under the supervision of:
Dr. Heba Abdel Aziz, Head of the General Administration of Museum Education for People with Special Needs in the Museums Sector.

As part of efforts to promote archaeological and cultural awareness among students from various universities, the team at the Graeco-Roman Museum is honored to organize a guided tour for students from Cardiff University. The tour aims to introduce them to the museum's rich and diverse collection, showcasing the historical significance of the Greco-Roman era in Egypt. The museum's modern display scenario, featuring advanced presentation, development, and restoration techniques, highlights these treasures. The visiting students expressed great admiration for the museum's collection and its innovative exhibition approach.
