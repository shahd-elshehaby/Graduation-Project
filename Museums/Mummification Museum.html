Most Popular Pieces

Canopic Jars

A set of four canopic jars belonging to Wah-Ib-Re-Neferu, son of Psamtik. These jars were used to store the embalmed organs of the deceased and had lids shaped like the Four Sons of Horus. They are called canopic jars, named after the city of Canopus, one of the ancient towns in Abu Qir, Alexandria.

Mummified Ram

This mummified ram is wrapped in linen and features a cartonnage mask with a gilded face. The ram was a symbol of the god Khnum, who was believed to shape each newborn on a potter’s wheel. His cult center was located on Elephantine Island.

Statue of Osiris

Osiris was regarded as the god of the afterlife and, since prehistoric times, was linked to the annual Nile flood, making him also the god of agriculture, fertility, and resurrection

The statue of the goddess Nephthys

Nephthys is considered one of the most important protective goddesses of the deceased.

The mummy of a Nile tilapia fish

Found in a worker’s tomb in Esna, this mummified Nile tilapia, called "Akha" by ancient Egyptians, was sacred in Esna (Latopolis, meaning "City of Fish"). Its consumption was forbidden, and it appears in tomb art, especially in Meidum and Faiyum.

Statue and Mummified Cat

In ancient Egypt, cats symbolized the goddess Bastet, whose popularity grew during the Late Period, especially in Tell Basta (Zagazig). The statue is made of sycamore wood.

The Djed pillar

A symbol of stability and continuity, originally represented the pillars that held up the heavens in the Old Kingdom. Over time, it also came to symbolize the backbone of the god Osiris, reflecting themes of resurrection and eternal strength. This iconic symbol remains a powerful representation of endurance and divine support in ancient Egyptian culture.

The crocodile mummy

It represents the worship of Sobek, the god of water and fertility, particularly revered in Kom Ombo. Found in the Kom Ombo Temple, it reflects the ancient Egyptian practice of mummifying sacred animals to honor deities, showcasing Sobek's role as a protector and symbol of the Nile's vitality.

The mummy of Masaharti

The mummy of Masaharti, son of King Pinedjem I of the 21st Dynasty, is a highlight of the museum. Discovered in 1881 in the Deir el-Bahri cache, it represents the peak of mummification artistry. Masaharti, High Priest of Amun and Commander of the Army, died young in his father's 25th regnal year. The well-preserved mummy shows signs of obesity, with a red-coated face, extended hands, and a gold cover on one finger. His coffin, adorned with funerary scenes, titles him as High Priest of Amun-Ra and Supreme Army Commander, reflecting his religious and military legacy.

Activities

The Mummification Museum welcomed a group from the Ma'at Initiative for Archaeological Awareness and provided explanations about:
- The journey of the deceased in the afterlife.
- The harmony and beauty of colors in the 21st Dynasty.
- Amulets in Ancient Egypt.

Continuing the effective role of the Educational Department, a guided tour was conducted for visitors covering the following topics:
- Burial ceremonies after death.
- Methods and customs of burial.
- The beauty and harmony of colors in the 21st Dynasty.
- The journey of the deceased to Abydos, the burial place of the god 'Osiris'.
