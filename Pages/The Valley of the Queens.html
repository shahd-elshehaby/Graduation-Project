Report

The Valley of the Queens is located in the southern part of the Theban Necropolis, near modern-day Luxor. The ancient Egyptians called this valley "Ta-Set-Neferu," meaning "The Place of Beauty." It was dedicated to the burial of non-reigning queens, princesses, and princes of the ruling dynasties from the 19th Dynasty to the end of the 20th Dynasty, with some tombs dating back to the 17th Dynasty. The Valley of the Queens is an important archaeological site that reflects the art and religious culture of ancient Egypt.

Location and History:
The Valley of the Queens is situated in a mountainous area southwest of the Temple of Hatshepsut at Deir el-Bahri, on the west bank of the Nile River. The valley was primarily used during the New Kingdom period (circa 1550–1070 BCE), especially during the 19th and 20th Dynasties. It was part of the royal necropolis of the ancient city of Thebes, which was the capital of Egypt during that time.

Number of Tombs: The Valley of the Queens contains approximately 90 tombs that have been discovered so far. These tombs belong to queens, princesses, princes of the ruling dynasties, as well as other prominent figures in the royal court. However, not all tombs are complete or well-preserved, as some have suffered damage due to the passage of time and natural factors.

Main Tombs:
Tomb of Queen Nefertari (QV66):
The tomb of Queen Nefertari, the principal wife of Pharaoh Ramses II, is one of the most magnificent tombs in the Valley of the Queens. It is renowned for its colorful wall paintings depicting the queen worshipping the gods and preparing for the afterlife. The tomb is considered a masterpiece showcasing the skill of ancient Egyptian artists. Tomb of Prince Amun-her-khepeshef (QV55):
This tomb belongs to Amun-her-khepeshef, the son of Pharaoh Ramses III. The tomb features scenes of the prince worshipping the gods, along with texts from the Book of the Dead. Tomb of Queen Titi (QV52):
This tomb belongs to Queen Titi, the wife of a king from the 20th Dynasty. It contains depictions of the queen offering sacrifices to the gods. Tomb of Prince Khaemwaset (QV44):
This tomb belongs to Prince Khaemwaset, the son of Pharaoh Ramses III. It is adorned with scenes showing the prince receiving blessings from the gods.

Historical and Artistic Significance:
Art and Inscriptions:
The tombs in the Valley of the Queens are famous for their colorful wall paintings, which reflect the ancient Egyptians' religious beliefs about the afterlife. These paintings depict queens and princesses worshipping the gods and preparing for their journey to the afterlife. Funerary Rituals:
The tombs were decorated with texts from the Book of the Dead and scenes depicting funerary rituals and offerings to the gods. These inscriptions provide valuable insights into the religious beliefs and funerary practices of ancient Egypt.

Modern Discoveries and Preservation:
Many tombs in the Valley of the Queens were discovered during the 19th and 20th centuries, and efforts continue to explore more tombs and restore the wall paintings. Some tombs have suffered damage due to erosion and humidity, requiring ongoing efforts to preserve this cultural heritage.

Conclusion:
The Valley of the Queens is a unique archaeological site that reflects the history and religious culture of ancient Egypt. Through its colorful tombs and exquisite inscriptions, the valley offers a glimpse into the lives of queens, princesses, and princes who lived during the New Kingdom period. The Valley of the Queens remains a testament to the artistic creativity and spiritual beliefs of the ancient Egyptians and is worthy of exploration and study by researchers and history enthusiasts.
