Report

Qaitbay Citadel, located on the Mediterranean coast in Alexandria, is one of Egypt's most iconic historical landmarks. Built by Sultan Al-Ashraf Abu Al-Nasr Qaitbay in the 15th century, the citadel stands on the site of the ancient Pharos Lighthouse, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. The fortress was constructed to protect Alexandria from naval invasions and has since become a symbol of the city's rich history and architectural heritage.

Historical Background
The citadel was built between 882 AH (1477 CE) and 884 AH (1479 CE) by Sultan Qaitbay, a Mamluk ruler, to fortify Alexandria's coastline against potential Ottoman threats. It was constructed using stones from the ruins of the ancient Pharos Lighthouse, which had been destroyed by earthquakes centuries earlier. Over time, the citadel underwent several renovations, including significant updates during the reign of Muhammad Ali Pasha in the 19th century, to adapt it to modern military needs. Despite suffering damage during the British bombardment of Alexandria in 1882, the citadel was restored and preserved by the Egyptian Supreme Council of Antiquities in the 20th century.

Architectural Design
Qaitbay Citadel is a rectangular fortress surrounded by the sea on three sides, offering stunning views of the Mediterranean. The citadel features two defensive walls and a main entrance flanked by two towers. The northern tower, which is square-shaped with rounded corners, served as the primary defensive structure. It includes small watchtowers, arrow slits for archers, and a vantage point where guards could spot incoming ships from a day's distance.
Inside the citadel, there is a mosque that holds historical and architectural significance as one of the oldest known mosques in Alexandria, covering an area of 30 square meters. The citadel also includes barracks for soldiers, a bakery, a kitchen, and a mill, reflecting its role as a self-sufficient military stronghold.

Notable Features
Defensive Towers: The citadel's towers, particularly the northern one, showcase advanced military architecture of the Mamluk era.
The Mosque: A historic mosque within the citadel, representing early Islamic architecture in Alexandria.
Panoramic Views: The citadel offers breathtaking views of the Mediterranean Sea, making it a popular tourist destination.
Restoration Efforts: The citadel has been meticulously restored to preserve its historical and cultural significance.

Qaitbay Citadel is a testament to Alexandria's strategic importance throughout history and the architectural ingenuity of the Mamluk era. Its location, design, and historical significance make it a must-visit site for anyone exploring Alexandria. The citadel not only reflects Egypt's rich past but also stands as a symbol of resilience and preservation, attracting visitors from around the world to admire its beauty and learn about its storied history.

Sound and Light Shows
The iconic Qaitbay Castle in Alexandria offers a captivating Sound and Light Show that immerses visitors in an exhilarating journey through the rich and fascinating history of one of Egypt's most celebrated cities. Against the awe-inspiring backdrop of this historic fortress, the show combines advanced lighting technology, stunning visual projections, and compelling historical narration to breathe new life into the city's glorious past.
As visitors take their seats within the massive walls of the castle, the stone architecture transforms into a canvas of vibrant images, depicting Alexandria's pivotal role throughout history. The lights and projections span centuries, starting from the city's founding by Alexander the Great, through its era as a hub of learning and culture during the Hellenistic age, and extending to its influence during Roman and Islamic times.
The show highlights the architectural and cultural wonders that once defined the city, such as the legendary Lighthouse of Alexandria, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, and the mythical library that served as the intellectual heart of the ancient world. Through vivid and detailed projections, visitors can see these lost landmarks as if they still stand at the height of their splendor.
While uncovering Alexandria's history, the show is presented in multiple languages, narrating the lives of key figures who shaped the city's destiny, including its rulers, scholars, and thinkers who left an indelible mark on its history. It also sheds light on prominent stories like those of Cleopatra and Julius Caesar, alongside moments from Egypt's rich Islamic heritage.
