Report

Salah El-Din Citadel, located on Pharaoh's Island (Geziret Faraun) in Taba, Sinai, is a historic fortress that stands as a testament to the strategic brilliance of Salah El-Din (Saladin) during the Ayyubid era. Built in 1170 CE, the citadel was constructed to secure the Red Sea and protect the pilgrimage route to the Hijaz. Its unique location on a coral island in the Gulf of Aqaba, approximately 250 meters from the Sinai coast and 10 kilometers from the port of Aqaba, makes it one of the most significant historical and archaeological sites in the region.

Historical Background
Pharaoh's Island, also known as Coral Island or Gray Island, has been a strategic location since ancient times. The island gained prominence during the Byzantine era when Emperor Justinian built a lighthouse in the 6th century CE to guide trade ships in the Gulf of Aqaba. However, the island's true significance emerged during the Crusader era when it was occupied in 1116 CE. Salah El-Din recognized its strategic importance during his campaign in Syria in 1171 CE and ordered the construction of the citadel to secure the region.
The citadel was built using granite stones quarried from the island itself. It was designed with two main fortifications—northern and southern—each capable of independent defense. The northern fortification, larger and more detailed, remains well-preserved, while the southern fortification sits on a smaller hill. Between the two lies a central plain housing storage facilities, a bakery, and other service units.

Architectural Features
The citadel's design reflects advanced military architecture of the Ayyubid period. Key features include:
Defensive Walls and Towers: The citadel was surrounded by an outer wall with nine defensive towers, though only one tower near the western dock remains today.
Water Reservoirs: Ingeniously carved into the rock, these reservoirs ensured a steady water supply during sieges.
Military Facilities: The citadel housed a weapons factory, a military meeting hall, and barracks for soldiers.
Religious Structures: A mosque built by Prince Hussam El-Din Bejla, as well as a Byzantine church discovered during excavations in 1988–1989, highlight the site's religious significance.
Byzantine Lighthouse: The remains of a Byzantine lighthouse on the southern hill underscore the island's historical role in maritime trade.

Excavations and Restoration
The Egyptian Antiquities Authority conducted extensive excavations in 1986, uncovering the foundations of towers, defensive structures, and religious buildings. Restoration efforts used original stones from the citadel, preserving its historical authenticity. Notable discoveries include two foundation plaques from the Ayyubid era, one commemorating the construction of a weapons factory and the other detailing the fortification walls.

Cultural and Historical Significance
The citadel played a crucial role in securing Egypt's eastern borders and protecting trade and pilgrimage routes. Its strategic location and robust design made it a key military outpost during the Ayyubid era. The discovery of Greek inscriptions and Byzantine artifacts further highlights the island's long history as a crossroads of civilizations.
In 1986, the Egyptian Antiquities Authority published a comprehensive book on the citadel's history and architecture, which also served as evidence of Egypt's historical claim to Taba. Recent renovations, including a modern lighting system, have enhanced the citadel's appeal, making it a must-visit destination for history enthusiasts and tourists.

Salah El-Din Citadel on Pharaoh's Island is a remarkable example of medieval military architecture and a symbol of Egypt's rich historical heritage. Its strategic location, advanced design, and historical significance make it a unique landmark in Sinai. Today, the citadel stands as a proud reminder of Egypt's past, offering visitors a glimpse into the region's storied history and the legacy of Salah El-Din's visionary leadership.
