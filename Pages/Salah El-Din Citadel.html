Report

Saladin's Citadel is one of the most important landmarks of Islamic Cairo and one of the oldest fortified castles built in the medieval era. Its strategic location atop the Muqattam Hill provides a magnificent panoramic view of all the historical sites of Cairo, while its strong defensive walls have offered additional protection to the capitals of Islamic Egypt against any external attacks.

History of Construction: Saladin began building this citadel on top of Muqattam Hill in 572 AH / 1176 AD at a site known as "Qubbat al-Hawa" (the Dome of the Wind), but he did not complete it during his lifetime. It was finished by Sultan Al-Kamil bin Al-Adil in 604 AH / 1207 AD, who became the first ruler to reside there and use it as the royal palace. The citadel remained the center of governance in Egypt until the reign of Khedive Ismail, who moved the seat of power to Abdeen Palace in modern Cairo.

Historical Significance: The citadel has witnessed numerous significant historical events from the Ayyubid era until the end of the Muhammad Ali dynasty, which ruled Egypt from the 19th century until the July 1952 Revolution. Over the centuries, many architectural additions were made to the citadel, including:

Muhammad Ali Mosque : One of the most beautiful mosques within the citadel, reflecting Ottoman architectural style.
Al-Nasir Muhammad Mosque : A masterpiece of Mamluk architecture.
Suleiman Pasha Mosque : An example of Ottoman military architecture.
In addition, the citadel houses several museums, such as:

Jewel Palace Museum : Displaying a collection of royal artifacts.
Police Museum : Narrating the history of the Egyptian police force.
Military Museum : Featuring exhibits related to various Egyptian wars.
